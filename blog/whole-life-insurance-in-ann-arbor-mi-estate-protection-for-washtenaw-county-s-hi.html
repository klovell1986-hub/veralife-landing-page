3. Ann Arbor's Financial Landscape and Whole Life Strategy

Why whole life works for Washtenaw County's high-income earners:
  • Retirement account saturation: High earners hit 401(k) and IRA contribution limits quickly. Whole life offers additional tax-advantaged savings space.
  • Estate planning at Michigan's exemption level: Michigan does not have a state estate tax, but federal estate taxes apply above $13.99 million (2026). Whole life in an irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT) provides tax-free liquidity for those above the threshold.
  • Philanthropic planning: U-M donors often name the university as a partial beneficiary of a whole life policy, creating a philanthropic legacy while maintaining coverage for family.
  • College funding for children/grandchildren: Cash value can be accessed via policy loans for education expenses without triggering taxable events.

FAQ — Ann Arbor Whole Life Insurance

Q: Is whole life better than term plus investing the difference?

A: For high earners who have already maxed retirement accounts, whole life provides unique benefits: guaranteed cash value, permanent coverage, and tax-favored growth. For others, term life plus investing may be more appropriate. The right choice depends on your income level, estate planning needs, and risk tolerance.

Q: Can I access the cash value tax-free?

A: Policy loans are not taxable as long as the policy stays in force. Withdrawals up to your cost basis are tax-free. Surrendering the policy triggers tax on gains above cost basis.

Q: Can I use whole life for business purposes?

A: Yes. Whole life is commonly used for key person insurance, buy-sell funding, executive bonus plans, and non-qualified deferred compensation in Michigan businesses.

Q: How are dividends taxed?

A: Dividends on participating whole life policies are considered a return of premium and are not taxable as long as they do not exceed the policy's cost basis.
