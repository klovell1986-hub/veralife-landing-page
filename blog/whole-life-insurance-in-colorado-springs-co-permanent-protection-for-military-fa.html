Colorado Springs-Specific Considerations

Colorado Springs is unlike any other Colorado city when it comes to whole life insurance planning. With five major military installations — Fort Carson, Peterson Space Force Base, Schriever Space Force Base, the U.S. Air Force Academy, and Cheyenne Mountain Space Force Station — over 40,000 active-duty personnel and their families call the Springs home. That's roughly 15% of the city's population directly connected to the military, and the percentage climbs when you include veterans, retirees, and defense contractor employees.

The whole life insurance need here is driven by a specific lifecycle event: the transition from active duty to civilian life. When a service member separates or retires, SGLI coverage ends within 120 days. They have the option to convert to VGLI at a premium that increases every five years — but many don't realize that a private whole life policy purchased during this transition window is almost always a superior long-term solution.

Colorado's state income tax doesn't apply to life insurance death benefits, and Colorado has no state estate tax. This makes Colorado a favorable jurisdiction for whole life policy ownership, whether the policy is owned individually or through an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT). For Colorado Springs families with TS/SCI clearances and stable government-contractor incomes, whole life provides the permanent, guaranteed coverage that aligns with long-term financial planning.

How to Get Coverage in Colorado

1. Assess your military-to-civilian coverage gap — If you're separating or retiring, SGLI ends 120 days after separation. VGLI conversion is automatic for 24 months but premiums increase every 5 years. Compare VGLI rates for the next 20 years against a level-premium whole life policy purchased now.

2. Choose the right face amount — For most Colorado Springs military families, $250,000–$500,000 in whole life provides permanent coverage that covers final expenses, creates a legacy, and builds cash value. Supplement with a term life policy for income replacement during working years.

3. Select a mutual carrier with strong dividend history — Mutual insurance companies have paid dividends to policyholders continuously for 100+ years in many cases. These dividends can buy paid-up additions that increase both the death benefit and cash value over time.

4. Consider an ILIT for estate planning — Military retirees with significant TSP balances and other assets should consider placing the whole life policy in an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust to keep the death benefit outside the taxable estate.

5. Work with an agent experienced in military underwriting — Some carriers have specific guidelines for deployment history, combat exposure, and PCS moves. VeraLife's licensed agents in Colorado understand military-specific underwriting.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Should I convert my SGLI to VGLI or buy a private whole life policy?

A: For most separating service members, buying a private whole life policy is the better long-term choice. VGLI premiums double every 10–12 years and build no cash value. A whole life policy purchased in Colorado Springs during the VGLI conversion window (within 2 years of separation) can be underwritten at your current health rating — which is often better than if you wait until VGLI becomes unaffordable at age 55–60.

Q: Can I get whole life insurance in Colorado if I have a deployment-related medical condition?

A: Yes, but it depends on the condition. Service-connected disabilities rated at 0–30% by the VA typically don't affect life insurance underwriting. Higher disability ratings (50%+) or conditions like PTSD with active treatment, TBI, or chronic pain may result in rate surcharges. Some carriers specialize in military underwriting and offer more favorable terms for deployment-related conditions.

Q: Does whole life insurance make sense for an E-7 or E-8 near retirement?

A: Yes. An E-7 or E-8 retiring at age 40–45 with a military pension and TSP savings should consider a $100,000–$250,000 whole life policy. The premium is level for life, the cash value provides a supplement to TSP in down markets, and the death benefit ensures that survivors receive immediate, tax-free funds — important when survivor benefit plan (SBP) payments may take months to start.

Q: Is the cash value in a whole life policy accessible while I'm still alive?

A: Yes. After 3–5 years of premiums, whole life policies build cash value that can be accessed through policy loans (typically at 5–8% interest) or withdrawals (up to your cost basis, tax-free). This is particularly valuable for Colorado Springs military families who face PCS moves, deployment transitions, or emergency expenses between pay cycles.
