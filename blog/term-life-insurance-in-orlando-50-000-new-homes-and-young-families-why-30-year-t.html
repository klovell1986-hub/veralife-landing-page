Introduction

Orlando isn't just theme parks and tourists anymore. Central Florida has become one of the fastest-growing residential regions in the United States. Over 50,000 new home permits were issued across the Orlando metropolitan area in 2025 alone — a trend powered by young families fleeing higher-cost metros and relocating to the Sun Belt. With that surge in homeownership comes an equally urgent need: protecting the mortgage, the household income, and the family's future if the unexpected happens.

Term life insurance is the most straightforward, affordable, and effective tool for that job. And for Central Florida families buying into the suburban boom, a 30-year term policy is often the smartest financial move they'll make.

This guide covers the local data every Orlando homeowner should know, breaks down the cost of coverage at key ages, and explains why the 30-year term fits the Orlando homebuyer profile better than any alternative.

The Central Florida Home Boom: By the Numbers

In 2025, the Orlando metropolitan statistical area (MSA) — which includes Orange, Osceola, Lake, and Seminole counties — surpassed 50,000 single-family and multifamily building permits. That volume ranks among the top three metros nationally for new housing construction, trailing only Dallas-Fort Worth and Houston among large metros.

Why the surge?

  • Relocation from high-cost states. Young professionals and families from California, New York, Illinois, and the Northeast continue arriving, drawn by Florida's absence of state income tax and a lower cost of living relative to coastal hubs. According to migration data, Florida consistently ranks as the top destination for out-of-state movers, and Orlando is the second-most popular Florida destination behind only the Tampa-St. Petersburg corridor.
  • Remote work permanence. Companies that went fully remote or hybrid during the pandemic have cemented those policies. Workers who once had to live within commuting distance of New York or San Francisco can now buy a home in Lake Nona, Winter Garden, or St. Cloud for a fraction of the price. These are not just second homes — they are primary residences for people who have restructured their entire lives around Florida's lower cost and better climate.
  • Suburban expansion. The hottest ZIP codes aren't downtown — they're in the suburbs. Horizon West, Ocoee, Clermont, Kissimmee, and Apopka are all seeing double-digit population growth as new subdivisions fill with families. On a typical Saturday, there are dozens of open houses in the area; on a typical Monday, a dozen new families are closing on homes they'll occupy for the next decade. The suburbs west of Orlando — along the State Road 429 corridor — are growing fastest, with entire master-planned communities rising from what was rural pastureland just five years ago.

For every one of those families, a mortgage has been signed. And for most of them, the primary earner's income is the only thing keeping that mortgage paid. The typical new Orlando mortgage ranges from $280,000 to $450,000 depending on the neighborhood, and the monthly payment — including property taxes, insurance, and HOA fees — can run anywhere from $2,000 to $3,500 per month. Losing the primary income would be devastating for most families within three to four months.

Disney, UCF, and the Orlando Employment Landscape

Orlando's employment base is distinct from other fast-growing metros. While cities like Nashville or Charlotte lean heavily into finance and healthcare, Orlando's economy splits into three major pillars:

1. Hospitality, Tourism, and Entertainment. Walt Disney World alone employs over 75,000 people in Central Florida, making it the largest single-site employer in the state. Universal Orlando employs approximately 25,000. SeaWorld and the surrounding hotel and restaurant ecosystem account for tens of thousands more. Combined, the tourism sector drives roughly 25% of all jobs in the region. The critical detail for life insurance planning: a significant portion of these jobs are part-time, seasonal, or contracted through third-party staffing agencies. When you are a performer, merchandise associate, or food-and-beverage worker at a theme park, you frequently fall outside the full-time employee category that qualifies for group life insurance benefits. Even for full-time W-2 employees at these companies, the employer-provided life insurance is typically capped at one or two times annual salary — often $50,000 to $100,000 in coverage, which is far too little to protect a $400,000 mortgage and a young family. 2. Healthcare. AdventHealth and Orlando Health together employ over 50,000 people in the region. Healthcare workers tend to have better employer-sponsored life insurance, but the same limitation applies: group life caps at one to three times salary, and coverage terminates when you leave the employer. For the many nurses, technicians, and administrators who move between Orlando's competing hospital systems, a personal term policy ensures continuous coverage through job transitions. 3. Education and Technology. The University of Central Florida (UCF) has one of the largest student bodies in the country — over 70,000 students — and employs roughly 12,000 faculty and staff. The surrounding tech ecosystem, anchored by Lockheed Martin, Siemens, and L3Harris, adds tens of thousands of professional jobs. These roles tend to offer better benefits packages, but even here, the group life coverage is rarely adequate for a family with a $400,000 mortgage and young children.

Why a 30-Year Term Policy Fits the Orlando Profile

Here is the fundamental math: if you are 35 years old with a 30-year mortgage, you will not pay off that house until you are 65. A 30-year term life insurance policy aligns perfectly with that timeline. It covers the years when your financial obligations are highest — the mortgage, childcare, college savings, and day-to-day living expenses.

A 20-year term leaves a dangerous gap. If you take out a $500,000 policy at age 35 on a 20-year term, that policy expires at age 55 — a full decade before the mortgage is scheduled to end. If you die between 55 and 65, your family inherits a house with a balance still due, no insurance payout, and a sudden income gap. That scenario — the 10-year gap — is where families get financially broken. A 30-year term closes it.

Furthermore, many Central Florida homebuyers are in their late 20s and early 30s. If you buy your first home at 30, a 30-year mortgage takes you to 60. A 30-year term policy purchased at 30 covers your family through the entire mortgage period plus a few additional years. The premium is locked — it will not increase, even if your health deteriorates, even if you change jobs, even if you move to another state. That predictability is one of the most valuable features of a level term life insurance policy.

Health and Underwriting Factors Specific to Orlando

Florida ranks near the middle nationwide for life expectancy at roughly 77 years, but Central Florida has specific health patterns that affect life insurance underwriting. Understanding these factors can help you prepare before you apply for coverage:

  • Obesity prevalence. Orange County's adult obesity rate hovers around 28%, roughly in line with the Florida state average. Excess body weight, measured by BMI, is one of the top factors underwriters use to determine premium classification. A BMI below 30 typically positions an applicant for preferred rates, while a BMI above 30 may result in standard or even substandard ratings.
  • Chronic conditions. Florida has higher-than-average rates of hypertension and heart disease, conditions that underwriters weigh carefully. If you have elevated blood pressure, taking steps to manage it — regular exercise, dietary changes, medication compliance — can significantly improve your rate classification.
  • Tobacco use. Florida's smoking rate is roughly 15%, below the national average, but underwriters treat any nicotine use (including vaping, cigar use, and cessation aids like gum or patches) as tobacco. A tobacco user will pay approximately two to three times the premium of a non-smoker at the same age.
  • Hurricane stress. While not a direct underwriting factor, the stress and economic disruption caused by hurricane seasons can indirectly affect health metrics. Proactive wellness management — staying active, managing stress, keeping up with annual physicals — is always beneficial, both for health and for life insurance rates.

The good news: being in a generally warm-weather state with year-round outdoor activity options is a net positive for wellness. Orlando residents have access to hundreds of parks, miles of paved trails, and outdoor recreation through the city's extensive parks system. Applicants who maintain healthy habits consistently qualify for preferred-tier rates.

One additional note: hurricane risk is real, and flood insurance is separate from homeowners insurance. A personal life insurance policy is one of the few forms of protection that has no deductibles, no exclusions for natural disasters, and no claim denials due to weather events. When a hurricane damages your property, your life insurance pays out exactly what it says on the declaration page — no depreciation, no waiting period for claims adjustment.

What About Employer-Provided Life Insurance?

Many Orlando workers assume their employer's group life insurance is sufficient. For most people, it is not. Here is why:

  • Coverage limits are low. Typical group life insurance caps at $50,000 to $150,000, regardless of the employee's salary. Even at companies with more generous benefits, the cap rarely exceeds two times salary. At a median Orlando-area salary of $55,000 to $65,000, that is $110,000 to $130,000 in coverage — enough for funeral expenses and a few months of mortgage payments, but not enough to pay off a home and support a family long-term.
  • Coverage ends when employment ends. If you leave your job — voluntarily or not — your group life insurance terminates. If you have a health condition at that point, you may be unable to qualify for a new individual policy at an affordable rate. This is known as "job lock" for life insurance, and it is a real risk in a region with high hospitality-sector turnover.
  • Portability is limited. Some employers offer the option to convert or port group coverage to an individual policy when you leave, but the converted rate is usually significantly higher than what you would pay for an equivalent individual policy purchased while healthy.

The recommended approach: carry enough individual term life insurance to cover your mortgage, outstanding debts, and at least five to seven years of income replacement. Treat your employer-provided coverage as a supplemental bonus — not your primary protection.

Conclusion: The Takeaway for Orlando Families

Orlando's growth story is real. Fifty thousand new homes in a single year means tens of thousands of families are entering the housing market, signing mortgages, and building lives in Central Florida. That growth brings excitement and opportunity — but it also carries the responsibility of making sure those families are protected.

If you have a 30-year mortgage and a family depending on your income, your life insurance term should match that mortgage term. A 30-year level term policy is not the most exciting financial product. It is, however, one of the most important — and at the prices available to healthy young applicants in 2026, it is more affordable than most people expect.

An ounce of planning now prevents a pound of financial stress later.

The Texas Property Tax Factor: Why It Makes Life Insurance More Valuable

One consideration that is unique to Texas — and especially important for San Antonio homeowners — is the property tax burden. Texas has no state income tax, but it has among the highest property tax rates in the country. San Antonio's effective property tax rate ranges from approximately 2.0% to 2.5% of assessed home value annually. On a $300,000 home, that is $6,000 to $7,500 per year in property taxes alone.

What does this have to do with life insurance? Everything.

  • Higher property taxes mean a larger monthly housing payment — even after the mortgage principal and interest have been budgeted. The monthly tax burden on the typical San Antonio home adds $500 to $625 per month to housing costs. In many cases, property taxes represent a larger portion of the monthly payment than the principal component.
  • If the primary earner dies, the family inherits not only the mortgage payment but also the ongoing property tax obligation. A surviving spouse who loses the primary household income may be unable to afford the combined mortgage-plus-tax bill, potentially forcing a sale of the home at exactly the worst possible time — during a period of grief and financial transition.
  • Term life insurance proceeds are generally income-tax-free to the beneficiary under Section 101(a) of the Internal Revenue Code. Those proceeds can cover the mortgage payoff and create a reserve for future property taxes, or they can provide income replacement that covers both the mortgage and tax burden. This gives the surviving family flexibility to stay in the home without financial distress.

In other words, in a high-property-tax state like Texas, the value of adequate life insurance coverage is even greater than in states with lower property tax burdens. A $500,000 policy that covers a $300,000 mortgage leaves $200,000 for property taxes, living expenses, and emergency savings — a buffer that can make the difference between a surviving family staying in their home and being forced to sell.

Military and Veteran Families: A San Antonio Priority

San Antonio's identity is inseparable from its military community. Joint Base San Antonio — encompassing Lackland Air Force Base, Fort Sam Houston, and Randolph Air Force Base — supports over 80,000 active-duty personnel and employs tens of thousands of civilians and contractors. The military family profile — often young, with children, frequent relocations, and deployment-related risk — creates specific life insurance needs that require more than a one-size-fits-all solution.

  • SGLI (Servicemembers' Group Life Insurance) provides up to $500,000 of coverage for active-duty members. It is affordable at roughly $29 per month for the maximum $500,000 in coverage, and enrollment is automatic. However, SGLI coverage does not follow a veteran after separation from service. Within 120 days of separation, it converts to VGLI (Veterans' Group Life Insurance), which becomes significantly more expensive as the veteran ages. A 35-year-old separating from active duty who converts to VGLI will pay roughly $100 per month for $500,000 in coverage.
  • The coverage gap for military families. SGLI coverage is limited to $500,000. For military families with a non-working or lower-earning spouse, $500,000 of income replacement may not be sufficient to cover a 30-year mortgage, child-rearing costs for multiple children, and college education expenses over two decades. A supplemental term life insurance policy purchased through the private market can address this gap at competitive rates.
  • Veterans transitioning to civilian employment often experience a gap between the end of SGLI coverage and the start of employer-provided benefits in a new civilian role. This gap can last 90 days or longer — and during that period, the family has no life insurance protection at all if the service member relied solely on military coverage. A personal term policy purchased while still on active duty ensures continuous coverage across this transition.
  • Spousal coverage. Military spouses often face challenges obtaining affordable life insurance due to frequent relocations, gaps in employment, and inconsistent access to healthcare. A level term policy for the spouse, purchased while both are healthy, provides portable, guaranteed coverage that follows the family through PCS moves.

For San Antonio's large veteran and military-affiliated population — which numbers well over 100,000 veterans in Bexar County alone — a 30-year term policy purchased during active service or shortly after separation provides affordable, portable coverage that does not depend on employer benefits or military status.
