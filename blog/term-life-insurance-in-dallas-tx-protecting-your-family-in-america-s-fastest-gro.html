Dallas-Specific Considerations

DFW is building homes faster than any other US metro — and new-home construction was up 14% year-over-year heading into 2026. From Celina and Prosper in Collin County to Mansfield and Midlothian in Johnson County, master-planned communities are opening new sections monthly. Every one of those new homes comes with a 30-year mortgage, a dual-income family depending on both paychecks, and a group life insurance benefit that covers roughly 2–4 months of expenses.

The Texas tax structure amplifies the risk. With no state income tax but an average effective property tax rate of 2.1% — one of the highest in the country — DFW homeowners face a unique financial squeeze. A family buying a $400,000 home in Dallas County pays roughly $8,400/year in property taxes. If the primary earner dies, the surviving spouse must cover both the $2,600–$3,200/month mortgage payment AND the property taxes — plus all other household expenses — on a single income. This is why life insurance sizing in Texas must account for the full cost of homeownership, not just the mortgage balance.

DFW's economy adds another layer. The region's growth is driven by corporate relocations (Goldman Sachs, Charles Schwab, Toyota, McKesson, CBRE) that brought tens of thousands of well-compensated professionals. These workers typically have employer-provided group life at 1× salary — $100,000–$200,000 — which sounds substantial until you realize their family needs $1M–$1.5M. The group coverage fills 10–15% of the need. A personal term policy closes the rest.

How to Get Coverage in Texas

1. Calculate your full need — For a DFW homeowner, don't just calculate the mortgage balance. Factor in 2.1% property taxes on the home's full value, private mortgage insurance (if under 20% equity), and 5–7 years of income replacement. For a $90,000 earner with a $400,000 home, that's roughly $1M in total need.

2. Choose 30-year term for mortgage alignment — If you bought your home in 2022–2025 with a 30-year mortgage at 6.5–7%, a 30-year term policy aligns with your mortgage payoff date. The extra $15–$20/month over a 20-year term prevents a coverage cliff when the 20-year policy expires but the mortgage is still 10 years from payoff.

3. Apply with simplified underwriting — Texas simplified issue policies up to $1M require no medical exam — just a short health questionnaire. Approval takes 24–72 hours, and your rate is locked for the full term.

4. Add waiver of premium rider — For $2–$5/month, this rider keeps your policy in force if you become totally disabled and can't work. Essential for self-employed DFW professionals and gig economy workers.

5. Review coverage after major life events — New home, promotion, new baby, or career change — each event should trigger a coverage review. VeraLife can help you adjust coverage without losing your original health rating.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does Texas's high property tax rate affect term life insurance premiums?

A: No. Life insurance premiums are based on age, health, and tobacco use — not where you live or local tax rates. However, Texas's property tax burden should affect how much coverage you buy. A DFW homeowner needs a larger death benefit than someone in a low-property-tax state because the surviving family must cover those annual tax payments indefinitely.

Q: Can I get term life insurance in Dallas if I'm self-employed or a gig economy worker?

A: Absolutely. In fact, self-employed workers need term life more than corporate employees because they have no employer-provided group coverage. Simplified underwriting (no exam) makes it easy to apply online in under 10 minutes. Your income doesn't need to be verified — your premium is based on age and health, not earnings.

Q: What happens to my term life policy if I move from Dallas to another state?

A: Your policy is fully portable — it stays in force regardless of your state of residence. Your premium and death benefit are locked in at issue. Just notify the carrier of your address change. This is especially relevant for DFW's transient workforce, which includes many corporate transferees and relocating professionals.

Q: Does Texas have a state income tax on life insurance death benefits?

A: No. Texas has no state income tax, and life insurance death benefits are exempt from federal income tax under IRC Section 101(a). Your beneficiaries receive the full death benefit with no income tax — federal or state — regardless of the policy size.
