Columbus-Specific Considerations

Columbus is undergoing an economic transformation that rivals any Midwest city. The 15,000+ new small businesses registered in 2025 span fintech, logistics, advanced manufacturing, and healthcare services — and most of their founders are first-time employers who have never set up a group benefits plan. Health insurance gets handled (often through a PEO or ICHRA), but group life, AD&D, and voluntary benefits are routinely overlooked until an employee specifically requests them or a key hire makes coverage a condition of their offer.

Nationwide Insurance’s global headquarters in Columbus employs ~12,000 people and has created a deep talent pool of insurance-adjacent workers — actuaries, underwriters, and benefits professionals who expect their own employer’s benefits to be professionally structured. Ohio State University’s 40,000+ employees set a similarly high standard. Small and mid-market employers competing for talent from these pools need benefits packages that at minimum include basic group term life and AD&D.

Intel’s $20 billion semiconductor campus under construction in Licking County (just east of Columbus) adds another dimension. The project is creating 3,000 Intel jobs and 7,000+ construction positions, pulling workers from across Central Ohio. Small businesses popping up to serve the Intel ecosystem — logistics, hospitality, professional services — need scalable benefits to attract and retain skilled workers in a tightening labor market.

How to Get Coverage in Ohio

1. Assess your current gap — If you offer health insurance through a PEO or ICHRA, check whether group life and AD&D are included or need to be added as a separate benefit

2. Decide employer-paid vs. voluntary — Employer-paid 1× salary sends the strongest retention signal; voluntary plans cost $0 to the employer while still offering meaningful coverage

3. Get multiple carrier quotes — Ohio has a competitive market with The Standard, Prudential, MetLife, Unum, and Lincoln all actively writing group business; work with a broker who represents multiple carriers

4. Set up enrollment — Online enrollment platforms with semi-annual open enrollment windows keep administrative burden low

5. Review at renewal — Group demographics change; carrier changes and new voluntary benefit offerings can improve cost and coverage over time

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What’s the minimum group size for group life insurance in Ohio?

A: Most carriers offer group life for groups as small as 2–3 eligible employees in Ohio. Participation requirements range from 75% (groups under 10) to 25–60% (groups of 10+). Very small groups may use simplified issue with a few health questions.

Q: Does Ohio have special rules for group life insurance?

A: Yes. Ohio’s small employer rating reform (HB 248, effective 2022) limits annual premium increases to 25% for groups of 2–50 employees. All group policies must be placed with an Ohio Department of Insurance-licensed carrier. Conversion rights are available for departing employees in groups of 10+.

Q: My company uses an ICHRA for health. Can we still offer group life?

A: Absolutely. ICHRAs cover health reimbursement only — group life and AD&D are separate lines of coverage. Columbus employers using ICHRAs can add voluntary or employer-paid group life through a direct-to-carrier policy, association benefits, or a voluntary platform like Colonial Life or Aflac.

Q: Is AD&D really worth adding for a professional services firm?

A: Yes. AD&D costs $1–$4/employee/month and covers accidents — the third-leading cause of death in the US. For service firms, it covers vehicle accidents during client visits, travel, and everyday activities. For a 25-person firm, the total annual cost for basic AD&D is roughly $300–$600.
