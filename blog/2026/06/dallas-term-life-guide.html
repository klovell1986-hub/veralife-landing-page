This guide covers term life costs in Dallas, top carriers available in Texas, coverage calculations for North Texas families, and how to buy.

1. What Is Term Life Insurance and Why Dallas Families Need It

Term life insurance is straightforward: you lock in a fixed premium for a set period — most commonly 10, 20, or 30 years — and if you pass away during that term, your beneficiaries receive a tax-free death benefit. No investment component, no complexity — just pure protection during the years your family needs it most.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I take my term life policy if I move out of Dallas or Texas?

Yes. Term life insurance is portable nationwide. If you relocate for a job transfer to Austin, Charlotte, or anywhere else, your policy stays in force as long as you continue paying premiums. VeraLife Insurance Group (NPN 21426840) serves clients across the U.S.

Can I convert my term life policy to permanent coverage?

Most term policies offered through VeraLife include a conversion privilege. This allows you to switch to a whole life or universal life policy — typically without a medical exam — during a specified window (usually the first 5–15 years of the term).

What if I don't want a medical exam?

Several carriers offer accelerated underwriting for applicants under 50 with clean health histories. Protective Life and Banner/Legal & General are the strongest in Texas for no-exam approvals. VeraLife can pre-screen your eligibility.

Should I get a 20-year or 30-year term?

For most Dallas families under 40, the answer is 30-year term. A 30-year fixed mortgage, children who will be in college in 20+ years, and a peak earning window that extends into your 50s all favor the longer term.

Can I have more than one term life policy?

Absolutely. Layering multiple policies is a common strategy. For example: a 30-year $500k policy to cover the mortgage and a 20-year $300k policy for income replacement during peak child-rearing years.

Isn't my employer coverage enough?

Most employer group life policies in Texas provide 1–2x your annual salary — typically $50k–$150k. That covers final expenses but leaves a significant gap for mortgage payoff, income replacement, and education. Plus, if you leave your job, the coverage ends.
