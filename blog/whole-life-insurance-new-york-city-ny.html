Market Overview

New York City is the single largest concentration of high-net-worth households in the United States. According to the 2025 Capgemini World Wealth Report, the New York metro area is home to more than 340,000 households with investable assets above $1 million — more than the combined HNW populations of Los Angeles, Chicago, and Houston. The five boroughs contain more than $1.5 trillion in trust and estate assets, a figure that grows annually as the city's finance, tech, real estate, and professional services sectors continue to generate outsized wealth.

For these households, whole life insurance is not merely a financial product — it is an essential component of comprehensive estate planning. The New York estate tax exemption of $6.94 million per individual (2026, indexed) captures a far broader range of estates than the federal exemption of $13.61 million. An NYC household with a $10 million estate — a $3 million co-op on the Upper East Side, $4 million in retirement and investment accounts, and a $3 million vacation home in the Hamptons — faces a potential New York estate tax liability of approximately $540,000. Whole life insurance, owned by an irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT), provides the liquidity to pay this tax without forcing a fire sale of the family home or investment portfolio.

The need is particularly acute for NYC's four major wealth-holding demographics:

  • Finance executives — partners and managing directors at Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, and BlackRock with concentrated stock and deferred compensation
  • Real estate families — multi-generational owners of NYC apartment buildings, commercial properties, and development firms with illiquid asset portfolios
  • Professional services partners — equity partners at law firms (Cravath, Paul Weiss, Skadden, Davis Polk), accounting firms, and consulting firms with buy-sell obligations
  • Medical professionals — partners and practice owners at NYC's major hospital systems (NYU Langone, Mount Sinai, Columbia, Weill Cornell)

The New York Estate Tax Trap

New York's estate tax regime is one of the most punitive in the United States for HNW households, and it creates specific demand for whole life insurance as a liquidity vehicle:

  • Exemption: $6.94 million per individual (2026, adjusted for inflation)
  • Rate: Progressive, starting at 3.06% and reaching 16% on the portion above the exemption
  • Cliff provision: Estates exceeding 105% of the exemption ($7.29 million in 2026) lose the benefit of the exemption entirely, taxed on the full estate value
  • No portability: Unlike the federal estate tax, New York does not permit a surviving spouse to use the deceased spouse's unused exemption — the exemption is use-it-or-lose-it per individual

The practical consequence: a $10 million NYC estate pays approximately 8-10% effective NY estate tax rate, or $800,000-$1,000,000 in state tax alone — before any federal estate tax calculation. Whole life insurance held in an ILIT provides the tax-free liquidity to cover this liability at the first death.

Whole Life Policy Structures for NYC HNW Clients

ILIT-Owned Whole Life — The most common and effective structure for NYC residents with estates exceeding $6.94 million. The ILIT is the policy owner and beneficiary. At death, the death benefit is paid to the ILIT, which is outside the insured's taxable estate. The trustee then uses the proceeds to purchase assets from the estate (or lend to it), providing the liquidity needed to pay estate taxes. ILITs are especially important for NYC clients because the death benefit would otherwise be subject to New York estate tax if the insured owned the policy. Survivorship (Second-to-Die) Whole Life — A popular structure for married NYC couples. This policy insures both lives and pays at the second death, when the federal and state estate tax bill comes due. Premiums are lower than two individual policies, making it cost-effective for couples with combined estates of $10-$20 million. The policy is typically owned by a survivorship ILIT. Private Placement Whole Life (PPLI) — For ultra-HNW NYC clients ($25M+), PPLI offers a customized whole life structure with investment management flexibility. The cash value is invested in a separately managed account, potentially offering better returns than a traditional whole life general account. PPLI is particularly relevant for NYC hedge fund partners and private equity executives seeking tax-efficient asset accumulation within an insurance wrapper.

How to Get Coverage in New York

1. Determine your estate tax exposure. Work with a CPA or estate attorney to calculate your total estate value and potential NY estate tax liability at $6.94M exemption (2026). Consider both first-death and second-death scenarios.

2. Establish an ILIT. An irrevocable life insurance trust must be created before the policy is applied for. The trustee (typically a family member, trusted advisor, or corporate trustee like a bank trust department) owns the policy and controls the death benefit distribution.

3. Get carrier quotes. VeraLife works with all six approved carriers. Request illustrations showing guaranteed cash values, current dividend scales (for participating policies), and death benefit projections.

4. Complete paramedical exam. Whole life requires a full exam: blood, urine, EKG (for ages 60+), and medical records review. NYDFS regulations also require a suitability review.

5. Fund the policy with Crummey letters. For ILIT-owned policies, annual premium gifts should be structured with Crummey withdrawal powers to qualify for the annual gift tax exclusion ($18,000 per beneficiary in 2026).

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does New York's estate tax cliff provision make whole life insurance more important?

A: Yes. Estates exceeding 105% of the exemption are taxed on 100% of the value — not just the excess. This means a $7.3 million estate (just above 105%) loses the full $6.94M exemption and is taxed on the entire amount. Whole life ILIT liquidity is essential for covering this unexpected tax burden.

Q: Can NYC residents use survivorship whole life instead of individual policies?

A: Survivorship (second-to-die) WL is appropriate when the estate tax liability is expected at the surviving spouse's death. For married couples with combined estates above $13.88M (two exemptions), a survivorship policy owned by an ILIT is often more cost-effective than two individual policies. For estates between $6.94M and $13.88M, individual policies may be preferable.

Q: Are whole life death benefits subject to New York state income tax?

A: No. Life insurance death benefits are exempt from New York state income tax under NY Tax Law §612(c)(1). The benefit is received income tax-free at both the federal and state level if the policy is properly structured.

Q: What happens to my whole life policy if I move out of New York?

A: The policy remains in force and fully portable. Your premium and death benefit stay locked in. New York's estate tax no longer applies if you establish domicile in another state. However, the policy was issued under NYDFS regulations and remains governed by NY insurance law.

Q: Can I access the cash value in my whole life policy before I die?

A: Yes — through policy loans (typically 5-8% interest) or withdrawals up to your cost basis (tax-free). Outstanding loans reduce the death benefit. NYDFS requires clear disclosure of loan terms and interest rate methodology.
