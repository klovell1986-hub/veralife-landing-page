Philadelphia-Specific Considerations

Philadelphia County has an estimated 22% of its 1.5 million residents aged 65 or older — one of the highest senior concentrations among major US cities. Combined with a 17.3% senior poverty rate (nearly double the national average), the need for affordable final expense coverage is acute.

Pennsylvania compounds this challenge with its state inheritance tax — one of only six states that still levy one. While life insurance death benefits paid to a named beneficiary are generally exempt from the 4.5% inheritance tax on lineal descendants, the rest of an estate passing through probate is not. A Philadelphia senior with a home valued at $200,000, retirement accounts of $150,000, and personal property of $25,000 leaves adult children facing a $16,875 inheritance tax bill (4.5% on $375,000) — due within 9 months of death.

A $15,000–$25,000 final expense policy serves dual purposes: it covers funeral costs and provides the surviving children with immediate, tax-free cash to pay the inheritance tax without touching the home or retirement accounts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How does Pennsylvania's inheritance tax affect final expense planning?

Pennsylvania taxes inherited assets at 4.5% for direct descendants (children, grandchildren), 12% for siblings, and 15% for other heirs. Surviving spouses and charities are exempt. Life insurance proceeds paid to a named beneficiary are generally exempt from inheritance tax — making final expense insurance the most tax-efficient way to fund end-of-life costs and provide liquidity for inheritance tax payments.

Q: Can I have more than one final expense policy in Philadelphia?

Yes. Many Philadelphia seniors split coverage — one $10,000 policy for funeral costs payable to a spouse or adult child who handles arrangements, and a separate $10,000–$15,000 policy as an inheritance tax offset. Each policy is individually underwritten and can have different beneficiaries.

Q: Will a final expense policy affect my Medicaid eligibility in Pennsylvania?

Pennsylvania's Medicaid rules exempt life insurance policies with a face value of $15,000 or less from countable resources. If the cash value pushes the total above $15,000, it could affect eligibility. For Philadelphia seniors already on or near Medicaid, a $10,000 policy is the safest option.

Q: What's the difference between simplified issue and guaranteed issue final expense?

Simplified issue (level benefit) asks 5–15 health questions, provides full coverage from day one, and has the lowest premiums. Guaranteed issue accepts everyone ages 50–85 with no health questions, but has a 2-year graded death benefit — if death occurs from natural causes during the waiting period, beneficiaries receive only a refund of premiums plus interest. Guaranteed issue premiums are typically 30–50% higher per dollar of coverage.
