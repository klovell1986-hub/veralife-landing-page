FAQs

Q: What is the minimum group size for group life insurance in the area? A: Most carriers offer group life for groups as small as 2–3 eligible employees in most states. Minimum participation requirements vary — typically 75% of eligible employees for groups under 10, 60% for 10+. Very small groups may use simplified issue (a few health questions) or guaranteed issue up to a threshold. Q: Can employees take their group life insurance when they leave? A: Group term life is not portable — coverage ends when employment ends. However, most group policies offer conversion rights, allowing departing employees to convert to an individual policy without new health underwriting. Conversion is typically available within 31 days of termination, but the premium is based on the employee's age at conversion, often higher than a new individual policy for a healthy person. Q: How are group life insurance premiums taxed? A: Employer-paid premiums for the first $50,000 of group term life coverage are tax-free to the employee. Premiums for coverage above $50,000 are taxable to the employee as imputed income, reported on Form W-2. Employee-paid premiums (voluntary) are paid with after-tax dollars unless offered through a Section 125 cafeteria plan, which allows pre-tax payments. Q: Is AD&D coverage worth adding to a group life plan? A: AD&D (Accidental Death & Dismemberment) coverage costs only $2–$5 per employee per month and provides benefits for accidents — the third-leading cause of death in the US. For the area employers in manufacturing, logistics, construction, or field services, AD&D is particularly valuable. It covers not just death but also loss of limbs, sight, speech, or hearing.

The voluntary benefits blind spot:

Many Baltimore small-mid employers don't know that group term life and AD&D can be offered on a voluntary basis — meaning the employer pays nothing. Employees pay 100% of the premium through payroll deduction, and the employer gets the benefit of offering a valuable benefit at zero hard cost. The carrier minimum participation myth:

Some employers believe they need to guarantee a minimum participation rate before a carrier will write a group life policy. While carriers do have participation requirements (typically 10–25% participation for voluntary plans), the thresholds are much lower than most employers assume. A 50-employee firm only needs 5–12 enrolled employees to launch a voluntary group life program.

4. Maryland Regulatory Environment for Group Benefits

Maryland has specific insurance regulations that affect how group life and AD&D are structured:

Maryland Insurance Administration (MIA) oversight:

All group life insurance policies sold to Maryland employers must be issued by a carrier licensed by the MIA. Carriers must file rates and forms for approval. This means Baltimore employers should verify their broker is placing coverage with a properly licensed carrier — not all carriers approved in Delaware or Pennsylvania are automatically approved in Maryland.

Group size requirements:

Maryland law defines a "group" for group life insurance as:

  • Employer-employee groups: at least 2 employees at time of issue
  • Labor union groups: established collectively
  • Association groups: subject to specific MIA guidelines

Maryland does not have a minimum participation requirement in statute — carrier-specific minimums apply contractually but are negotiable with most major carriers.

Small employer rate bands:

For groups under 100 employees, Maryland follows NAIC model regulations requiring rate bands that stabilize premiums across small groups. This means a Baltimore biotech startup with 15 employees won't face the wild rate swings that might occur in unregulated markets. Rate bands must be filed with the MIA and are typically revised annually.

COBRA continuation:

Under Maryland law, group life insurance benefits of $50,000 or less do not require COBRA continuation (federal COBRA applies to group health only). However, some Maryland employers voluntarily offer conversion rights — allowing departing employees to convert group life to an individual policy without underwriting. This is not required by Maryland statute but is a common carrier offering.

ERISA compliance:

All employer-sponsored group life, AD&D, and voluntary benefits plans are subject to ERISA (federal Employee Retirement Income Security Act). This means:

  • Summary Plan Descriptions (SPDs) must be provided to participants
  • Annual Form 5500 filings may be required (for plans with 100+ participants)
  • Fiduciary obligations apply to plan administration

Baltimore employers should work with a benefits consultant or broker who handles ERISA compliance documentation — not all agents are ERISA-licensed.

Baltimore's status as a biosciences hub means thousands of employees work in environments with elevated accident risk:

Lab workers (Biosafety Level 1 and 2):
  • Needle sticks and sharps injuries
  • Chemical splash exposure
  • Biological aerosol exposure
  • Centrifuge and equipment accidents
Field researchers and clinical trial coordinators:
  • Travel to clinical sites and patient homes
  • Vehicle accidents during fieldwork
  • Slips, trips, and falls in clinical environments
Biomanufacturing and production:
  • Heavy equipment operation
  • Chemical handling (solvents, reagents)
  • Compressed gas cylinder accidents
  • Repetitive motion injuries

AD&D (Accidental Death & Dismemberment) coverage pays a lump-sum benefit for:

  • Accidental death
  • Loss of limb(s)
  • Loss of sight (one or both eyes)
  • Loss of speech or hearing
  • Permanent paralysis

4. Ohio Regulatory Environment for Group Benefits

Ohio has specific insurance regulations that affect group life and AD&D benefits:

Ohio Department of Insurance (ODI) oversight:

All group life insurance policies issued to Ohio employers must be placed with a carrier licensed by the ODI. Ohio is a rate-and-form filing state — carriers must submit rates and policy forms for ODI approval. Carriers may not deviate from filed rates without prior ODI approval.

Small employer rating reform:

Ohio enacted small employer rating reforms under HB 248 (effective 2022) that apply rate banding and renewal rating restrictions to group life insurance for employers with 2–50 employees. This provides rate stability for Columbus small businesses — premiums cannot increase more than 25% from one year to the next for groups in this size range, except under specific conditions (significant claims, demographic change, or coverage change).

Coverage for domestic partners:

Ohio law does not mandate domestic partner coverage for group life insurance, but many carriers offering coverage in Ohio do include domestic partners as eligible dependents. Columbus employers should verify with their carrier whether domestic partner coverage is available — carrier practices vary.

COBRA and conversion rights:

Group life benefits under $50,000 do not require COBRA continuation in Ohio (federal COBRA applies to health plans only). However, Ohio law does provide conversion rights: departing employees of groups with 10+ employees may convert their group life coverage to an individual whole life policy without evidence of insurability, subject to carrier conversion provisions. This is a valuable benefit for employees leaving a Columbus employer and should be highlighted in the SPD.

ERISA and state law interplay:

As with all employer-sponsored group benefits, ERISA governs plan administration, fiduciary obligations, and disclosure requirements. Ohio state law governs the insurance contract itself (policy forms, rates, insurer solvency). Columbus employers must comply with both frameworks — your broker should be ERISA-literate.

Workers' compensation in Ohio (BWC):

Ohio is one of four states (along with Wyoming, Washington, and North Dakota) that require employers to obtain workers' comp exclusively through the state-run Ohio Bureau of Workers' Compensation (BWC), unless they qualify for self-insurance. This means employers cannot use private carriers for workers' comp. However, group life and AD&D are not impacted by this requirement — they are distinct lines of coverage.
